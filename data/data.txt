Napoleon Bonaparte (15 August 1769 – 5 May 1821) was a French military and political leader who rose to prominence during the French Revolution and led a series of successful campaigns across Europe.

Early Life:
Napoleon was born on the island of Corsica to a family of Italian origin. He moved to mainland France in 1779 and was commissioned as an officer in the French Army in 1785. He supported the French Revolution in 1789 and rose rapidly through military ranks.

Rise to Power:
Napoleon gained national fame by breaking the siege of Toulon in 1793. In 1796, he commanded a brilliant campaign against Austria in Italy, scoring decisive victories and becoming a national hero. In November 1799, he seized power in a coup and became First Consul of France.

Emperor of the French:
In December 1804, Napoleon crowned himself Emperor of the French at Notre Dame Cathedral in Paris. He implemented sweeping reforms including the Napoleonic Code — a civil law system that influenced legal systems worldwide. He also reformed French education, administration, and established the Banque de France.

Military Campaigns:
Napoleon built a vast empire through military conquest. His greatest victory was at the Battle of Austerlitz in 1805, where he defeated the combined armies of Austria and Russia. He defeated Prussia at the Battle of Jena in 1806 and Russia at the Battle of Friedland in 1807. At its peak, his empire directly or indirectly controlled about 70 million people across Europe.

Decline and Defeat:
Napoleon's empire began to collapse after his catastrophic invasion of Russia in 1812, in which his Grande Armée of over 400,000 men was destroyed by the Russian winter and scorched-earth tactics. In 1813, a coalition of European powers defeated him at the Battle of Leipzig. Paris fell in 1814 and Napoleon was forced to abdicate. He was exiled to the island of Elba.

The Hundred Days:
In February 1815, Napoleon escaped from Elba and returned to France, rallying support and reclaiming power. The European powers formed a new coalition against him. He was finally and decisively defeated at the Battle of Waterloo on 18 June 1815 by the Duke of Wellington and the Prussian army under Blücher.

Final Exile and Death:
After Waterloo, Napoleon was exiled by the British to the remote island of Saint Helena in the South Atlantic Ocean. He spent his remaining years dictating his memoirs. He died on 5 May 1821 at the age of 51. The official cause of death was stomach cancer, though some historians have suggested arsenic poisoning.

Legacy:
Napoleon is considered one of the greatest military commanders in history. His Napoleonic Code remains the basis of civil law in France and many other countries. He modernized European legal and administrative systems, promoted equality before the law, abolished feudalism in conquered territories, and emancipated Jews and other religious minorities. However, he is also criticized for his authoritarian rule, the wars that devastated Europe, and for reinstating slavery in French colonies.

Key Facts:
- Born: 15 August 1769, Ajaccio, Corsica
- Died: 5 May 1821, Saint Helena
- Reign as Emperor: 1804 to 1814, and briefly in 1815
- Spouse: Joséphine de Beauharnais (1796–1809), Marie Louise of Austria (1810–1821)
- Children: Napoleon II (with Marie Louise)
- Notable battles: Austerlitz (1805), Jena (1806), Borodino (1812), Waterloo (1815)
